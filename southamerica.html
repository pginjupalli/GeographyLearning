Argentina:

    Location: Southern America
  • Capital City: Buenos Aires
  • Population: 45,808,747
  • Common Language(s): Spanish
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity, No Religion

Argentina transitioned to democracy in the 1980s after a period of military rule and has since grappled with economic challenges like inflation and debt. However, it remains one of South America's largest economies today from their beef and wine exports. Meanwhile, their culture thrives with the country's love for soccer that is well known and unites the nation.

Bolivia:

    Location: Central South America
  • Capital City: La Paz, Sucre
  • Population: 12,079,472
  • Common Language(s): Spanish, Quechua, Aymara, etc
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity

Bolivia is a landlocked country. Its economy is characterized significant mineral resources like natural gas, but also faces challenges related to poverty and income inequality. Interestingly, its historical narrative is marked by the ancient Inca civilization, political shifts, and social movements, culminating in the election of its first Indigenous president, Evo Morales, in 2006.

Brazil:

    Location: Eastern South America
  • Capital City: Brasília
  • Population: 214,326,223
  • Common Language(s): Portuguese
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity, No Religion

Brazil is known for its soccer passion and iconic Carnival celebrations. It boasts one of the world's largest economies, driven by agriculture, manufacturing, and natural resources, though it grapples with economic challenges and inequality. Politically, Brazil is a republic with a history of democratic transitions, but recent years have seen political polarization and concerns over environmental policies related to the Amazon Rainforest.

Chile:

    Location: Western South America
  • Capital City: Santiago
  • Population: 172,683
  • Common Language(s): Spanish
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity, No Religion

Chile is a long, narrow country on the western edge of South America. This nation boasts a stable and open economy, driven by copper exports, a strong wine industry, and a growing tourism sector. Also, folk music traditions like the cueca and a passion for football (soccer) are staples in Chilean culture. Meanwhile, its history is marked by a transition from military rule to democracy in the late 20th century.

Colombia:

    Location: Northern South America
  • Capital City: Bogotá
  • Population: 51,516,562
  • Common Language(s): Spanish
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity, No Religion

Colombia's economy includes things like agriculture and mining, and it has emerged as a popular destination for tourism and coffee exports. Notably, its culture is a mix of indigenous, African, and Spanish influences, celebrated through festivals such as Carnaval de Barranquilla. While Colombia has faced challenges such as political conflict and drug-related violence in the past, it has made significant strides toward peace and development in recent years.

Ecuador:

    Location: Northern South America
  • Capital City: Quito
  • Population: 17,797,737
  • Common Language(s): Spanish, Quechua, Schuar, etc
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity

Ecuador's economy is fueled by oil exports and tourism, but has an emphasis on preserving its unique ecosystems. Ecuadorian culture comes from indigenous, mestizo, and Spanish traditions due to the nation's history with the Incan Empire, Spanish colonization, and a struggle for independence. More recently, Ecuador has been recognized for its progressive stance on environmental conservation and indigenous rights.

Guyana:

    Location: Northern South America
  • Capital City: Georgetown
  • Population: 804,567
  • Common Language(s): English, Guyanese Creole
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity, Hinduism

The country's economy relies on industries like mining and petroleum, with significant reserves of oil. Guyanese culture reflects African, Indian, European, and Indigenous traditions due to its history is marked by Dutch and British colonial periods. However, they were one of the few countries to grow despite the pandemic. Guyana is also known for its pristine rainforests and the mighty Kaieteur Falls, one of the world's tallest single-drop waterfalls.

Paraguay:

    Location: Central South America
  • Capital City: Asunción
  • Population: 6,811,297
  • Common Language(s): Spanish, Guarani
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity

Paraguay is a landlocked country known for its Guarani heritage and stable economy. Agriculture, especially soybeans, plays a key role in its economy with the country being the 6th largest producer of soybeans. Paraguayan culture is a blend of Indigenous, Spanish, and Jesuit influences, mostly due to Indigenous civilizations and Spanish colonization. Today, Paraguay is recognized for the Guarani language and the country being an agricultural hub.

Peru:

    Location: Western South America
  • Capital City: Lima
  • Population: 33,715,471
  • Common Language(s): Spanish, Quechua, Aymara
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity

Peru is a nation with a complex blend of economy and culture. The country relies on mining, agriculture, and tourism, with its diverse geography, including the Amazon Rainforest. Peruvian culture is a rich from Indigenous, Spanish, and African influences, shown in dishes like ceviche. Recently, Peru has experienced political turmoil, including presidential impeachments and struggles for political stability.

  • Population: 612,985
  • Common Language(s): Dutch, Sranan Tongo, English

Uruguay:

    Location: Southern South America
  • Capital City: Montevideo
  • Population: 3,456,750
  • Common Language(s): Spanish, Uruguayan Sign Language (LSU)
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity, No Religion

Uruguay is known for its robust economy and progressive culture. The country's economy relies on agriculture, particularly cattle ranching, and a well-developed services sector. Venezuela:

    Location: Northern South America
  • Capital City: Caracas
  • Population: 28,199,867
  • Common Language(s): Spanish
  • Religions/Beliefs: Christianity, No Religion

Venezuela is culturally rich, with influences from Indigenous, African, and European traditions. However, the country now faces severe economic challenges like hyperinflation and shortages of basic goods due to economic mismanagement and falling oil prices. Politically, Venezuela grapples with a power struggle between the government and opposition, leading to international scrutiny and diplomatic tensions.
